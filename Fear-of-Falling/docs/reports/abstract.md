# Abstract

## Background and Purpose

Fear of falling (FOF) is common in older adults and may influence functional trajectories following fall prevention interventions. This study examined whether baseline FOF status was associated with differential 12-month changes in physical performance after participation in a multifactorial falls prevention program (MFFP), and whether functional reserve and deficit burden contextualized these associations.

## Methods

In this retrospective cohort study, 527 community-dwelling adults aged 65 years or older at elevated risk for falls participated in an MFFP. Physical performance was assessed at baseline and 12 months using four performance-based tests summarized as a composite z-score. FOF was measured using a validated single-item question. Longitudinal mixed-effects and covariance models were conducted in a common analytical sample of participants with complete 12-month follow-up (n = 236). Extended models incorporated locomotor capacity (latent functional reserve) and a frailty index (deficit accumulation) to evaluate independent explanatory contributions beyond canonical covariates. Primary model specifications were held constant in extended analyses.

## Results

Among participants with complete follow-up (mean age 78 +/- 6 years; 92% women), 72% reported FOF at baseline. Individuals without FOF demonstrated modest improvement in composite performance over 12 months, whereas those with FOF showed no significant change. In head-to-head models using the same analytical sample, locomotor capacity was more strongly associated with longitudinal performance trajectories, while the frailty index contributed additional but smaller independent explanatory information. Capacity and deficit burden were moderately correlated but not collinear.

## Conclusions

FOF is associated with differential performance trajectories following fall prevention. Functional reserve and accumulated vulnerability represent related yet non-redundant dimensions that help contextualize these associations. Interpretations are descriptive and non-causal.
