# Discussion

Frailty has traditionally been conceptualized through two major operational frameworks: the phenotype model, emphasizing physical attributes such as weakness and slowness, and the deficit accumulation model, operationalized as a frailty index reflecting cumulative health burden. Rather than treating these approaches as interchangeable, our findings support partial non-equivalence. In head-to-head analyses using a common analytical sample (n = 236 with complete 12-month follow-up), locomotor capacity and deficit accumulation were moderately correlated yet non-collinear, and each provided independent information on longitudinal performance trajectories. Capacity showed a stronger association with change over time, consistent with a functional reserve construct, whereas the frailty index reflected accumulated vulnerability that contextualized baseline risk and contributed additional, though smaller, trajectory information. Together, these findings support a multidimensional interpretation of frailty in which reserve and vulnerability are related but distinct dimensions of risk.

Within this framework, fear of falling remained clinically relevant as a baseline risk characteristic but did not show statistically robust time-moderation in fully adjusted head-to-head models. This pattern suggests that psychological status, reserve, and vulnerability likely capture overlapping but non-identical aspects of risk expression during follow-up, and that adjusted longitudinal change should not be attributed to a single domain alone. Accordingly, our interpretation is descriptive and non-causal.

The practical implication is not to replace one frailty operationalization with another, but to use complementary constructs to contextualize heterogeneity in response to multifactorial falls prevention. Capacity may better capture dynamic change potential, whereas deficit burden may better characterize baseline vulnerability context. This combined perspective can strengthen risk stratification and interpretation of longitudinal performance outcomes in older adults while preserving fear of falling as the primary clinical question in this study.
