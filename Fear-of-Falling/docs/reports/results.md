# Results

In adjusted head-to-head longitudinal models, both locomotor capacity and deficit burden provided independent information on performance trajectories. Higher baseline capacity was associated with greater improvement over 12 months (`time×capacity`: estimate `0.01409`, `p<0.001`), whereas higher frailty index was associated with less favorable change (`time×FI`: estimate `-0.01024`, `p<0.001`). These effects were observed simultaneously within the same model, consistent with non-redundant contributions of functional reserve and accumulated vulnerability.

In contrast, fear of falling did not significantly modify longitudinal change after covariate adjustment (`time×FOF`: estimate `-0.00287`, `p=0.60`). Capacity and FI were moderately correlated (`r≈-0.51`) but non-collinear, supporting simultaneous inclusion in the BOTH model.

Model-based trajectory visualizations are consistent with coefficient-based inference: the capacity gradient showed a stronger longitudinal slope difference, while FI showed an independent but smaller gradient. Main gradients (capacity and FI varied separately at `-1 SD`, `mean`, `+1 SD`) and supplementary extreme profiles are documented in K44 figure outputs.
